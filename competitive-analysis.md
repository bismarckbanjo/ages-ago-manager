# Retro Tech Apparel: Competitive Landscape
*Ages Ago Apparel — June 2026*

---

## The Field

The retro tech apparel space has **no dominant dedicated brand**. It's occupied by a mix of:

1. **Broad geek/developer stores** that include retro as one sub-category
2. **Pop culture generalists** that dabble in retro computing
3. **Unbranded marketplace noise** (Redbubble, TeePublic, Etsy, Amazon)

This is the core opportunity: **the category is unclaimed**.

### 3. Redbubble / TeePublic
**Marketplace noise, not brands.**

| | |
|---|---|

### 4. Etsy / Amazon / Merch by Amazon

## What You Should Do

### The Core Move: Own the category.
No one is the *brand* for retro tech nostalgia apparel. Ages Ago has the name for it. The goal is to be the obvious choice when someone thinks "I want a shirt that celebrates the computer I grew up with."

---

### Priority Actions

**1. Sharpen the brand story (do this first)**
TechGeeksApparel sells jokes. You should sell *memory*. The positioning is "this shirt is about where you were" — not just what's funny. Every product description, homepage, and social post should reinforce that emotional hook. The name "Ages Ago" is perfect for this — use it.

**2. Expand catalog depth strategically**
TechGeeksApparel wins on volume but not focus. You don't need 200 products — you need 40–60 *well-designed, specific* products that feel curated. Think: specific machines (Apple IIe, Commodore 64, TRS-80, Amiga), specific eras (BBS culture, shareware era, dial-up), specific aesthetics (boot screens, error dialogs, loading bars). Specificity beats breadth when you own a niche.

**3. Price confidently at $28–$38**
TechGeeksApparel is at $22–$32 with generic POD. If your quality and design are better, price accordingly. Don't race to the bottom. The nostalgia buyer is typically a 35–55-year-old with disposable income.

**4. Build a social presence around the community, not just the product**
Post the memories, not just the shirts. "What was your first computer?" outperforms product photos. The nostalgia niche *thrives* on community engagement — Redbubble and TechGeeksApparel can't do this because they have no identity. You can.

**5. Target SEO gaps**
"Commodore 64 shirt," "Apple II t-shirt," "retro computing apparel" — these are low-competition, high-intent searches. TechGeeksApparel ranks for broad terms; you can own the specific ones.
